Introduction

In my experience, the ability to bring together different people to not only coexist but also work alongside one another towards a common goal is what defines a leader. In my undergraduate degree at the University of British Columbia, I studied international relations and from my lessons in political science and history, this ability to lead individuals towards realizing a common goal is what has historically separated successful leaders from their failed counterparts. In self-reflecting, I can certainly recognize that the values and principles that guide my approach to leadership have certainly been shaped by my education, experiences and mentors, in addition to my family’s culture, traditions and the experiences of my parents and grandparents.

While my major in my undergraduate degree was in international relations, I did my minor in geographic computation. To offer some personality in addition to deeper insight into the geographic contexts that shaped my values and principles, I decided to program this digital storyboard to visualize both me and my family’s journey to the present.

This leadership charter first looks internationally and to the past, examining the history of my family to provide insight into the cultural context that has shaped my leadership values. Afterwards we will return back here, to Vancouver, to consider the experiences and individuals that have shaped my principles more directly and personally.

My Familial Context and Heritage

My family traces our heritage back to the Canton region of China, now known as Guangzhou. All of my grandparents were born in this region and lived there for the early years of their lives. For the most part, my grandparents came from mostly rural villages on the periphery of urban areas.

I have always found hearing the contrasting anecdotes between the family cultures of my grandmother and grandfather on my mother’s side fascinating and insightful into how my mother’s parents fostered our family’s culture when they had children of their own. Both my grandmother and grandfather were raised in different rural villages which shaped their own familial cultures.

According to my family’s anecdotes, my great grandfather on my grandmother’s side was famously greedy and stingy with his resources despite being a fairly wealthy man. In contrast, while being relatively poorer, my great grandfather on my grandfather’s side prioritized principles of generosity and humility within his children. Once my grandparents got married, similar to his father before him, my grandfather prioritized fostering a culture of generosity and humility amongst all his grandchildren. This culture still resonates in my approach to leadership. When I assume leadership positions in school, my work and extracurricular activities, I find leaders who exercise generosity and humility in their leading compelling and try to do the same when I’m a leader by ensuring that everyone has equal say in conversations and by deferring to others who are more knowledgeable in specific areas of knowledge even when I’m the leader in the broader context.

Arriving in South Africa

However, in the early decades following the turn of the 20th century, spurred by economic potential and growing political tensions and conflict, my family moved from Guangzhou, China to South Africa. Initially much of my family used to live in Johannesburg, however, on the several vacations I have taken to South Africa, we often go to Cape Town where much of my family is now situated.

When my family initially moved to South Africa, alongside a growing Chinese diaspora at the time, Asian, Black and White communities were all segregated into racialized neighborhoods known as “townships” by the racist and discriminatory systems in place at the time. For my family and many Black and Asian people, segregation created significant stress and anxiety. For much of my family, traveling outside their designated communities could make them subject to persecution and suspicion from law enforcement. This combined with the many other systemic disadvantages at the time meant that they spent much of their time inside their segregated communities.

However, despite facing such discriminatory challenges to them, I have always found my family’s story and their connection with the South African-Chinese community more broadly inspiring. While the apartheid rules dictated that the Chinese community living in South Africa were limited to living only in certain areas, my grandparents and the friends close to them took it as an opportunity to organize and build up their local communities. From organizing sports leagues, community dinners, and Mahjong and games nights, I find the story of the Chinese community in South Africa building up the connections in their neighborhoods inspiring.

This value of building community and camaraderie is key to how I lead. It is my belief that people work best when they collaborate, trust and build off of one another. When I assume leadership positions, I aim to build up that familiarity and foster positive relationships between people. I believe that community can act as a strong glue for groups that work together allowing individuals who usually wouldn’t be willing to work together to thrive through collaboration.

Both my parents were born in South Africa and lived there into their thirties. However, escalating racial tension in the country, especially during the 1980s led to my family looking elsewhere to settle. With my grandparents getting older, the escalating racial tensions in South Africa led to my family looking for a new home. In the late 1980s my parents would move to Vancouver, Canada soon followed by my grandparents.
